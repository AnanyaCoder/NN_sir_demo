How to read this
Each row is one beat of the film — the narration/on-screen text is the copy as it will appear, the Visual column is the stage direction for what's on screen, and Audio is music/SFX guidance. Rows flow in order; this is one continuous film, not separate teasers.

Text-to-visual timing follows the same default as the AI4B-Transcribe film: the line appears first to establish the claim, then the visual/demo rolls in underneath it while the text shrinks to a caption. Scenes 2 and 7 (the withheld-answer beat and the MCQ beat) are quick one-liners where text and visual can land together.

Scenes 1–8 are Student mode. Scene 9 is the turn. Scenes 10–13 are Teacher mode. Scene 14 closes. Platform and launch-date specifics are placeholders — fill in once confirmed; everything else is draftable now.

Demo topics in Scenes 2–8 are drawn one-per-beat from TutorBot's current live subject coverage — Photosynthesis, Algebra, Geometry, Newton's Motion, Force & Friction, Human Body, Light — so the film doesn't imply broader coverage than what's actually shipped. No topic repeats across the Student-mode beats.

Scenes 5, 6, and 8 now describe the actual product UI rather than a generic placeholder: the drag-to-explore interaction, the Atlas constellation graph (clustered nodes, mastery bar, zoom/reset controls), and the escalating hint-card format ("HINT – 1 · GENTLE NUDGE" through Hint 3) are all pulled from real captures. Whoever storyboards the actual screen recording should use these as the shot reference rather than re-imagining the UI.

Rows shaded grey under a scene are added multilingual examples — one code-mixed language demo per Student-mode learning-science beat (Hindi, Telugu, Kannada, Bengali, Konkani), so the film's core moments read as genuinely multilingual, not just English with a language-toggle claim. English is the base narration throughout. These lines are illustrative for storyboard purposes and should be checked with a native speaker of each language before use in production copy.

The withheld answer — Photosynthesis
Notice something? We didn't answer the question. "We asked one back." A student asks why plants need sunlight to make food — TutorBot doesn't explain. It asks what happens to a plant kept in the dark for a week.
Chat bubble: student asks "Why do plants need sunlight to make food?"; a beat of silence; then TutorBot's question appears instead: "What do you think happens to a plant kept away from sunlight for a week?"

3
Socratic demo — Algebra
A student stuck solving 2x + 3 = 11. TutorBot doesn't solve it. It asks what should happen to both sides of the equation first — then the next question, and the next — until the student isolates x themselves.
Chat log plays out in real time, each TutorBot turn highlighted as a question, not an answer, ending on the student's own correct line: x = 4
Continues, understated

4
Rooted in IKS — Geometry
But guiding isn't only asking questions. It's also showing a student where the idea came from. The Baudhayana Shulba Sutra stated the diagonal-of-a-rectangle relationship centuries before Pythagoras — and that's exactly where the lesson begins.
Split screen: the Shulba Sutra verse on the rectangle's diagonal dissolving into the NCERT Class 9 lesson on the Pythagoras theorem that uses it
Theme shifts — warmer, more rooted
5
Active learning — Newton's Motion
Some things you don't explain. You let a student play with them. A student drags the incline steeper and watches a ball's speed change on its way down.
The product's real drag-to-explore pattern: a labelled ramp diagram with a draggable incline-angle arrow; as it moves, a live stats row updates underneath (angle, speed, time to bottom), and a short tip callout explains what just changed — same structure as the in-product Light simulations, applied here to motion
Playful, curious register

6
Atlas — the concept graph — Force & Friction
Every student gets stuck somewhere different. TutorBot knows exactly where. A student stuck on a friction problem is quietly traced back to an unresolved "Normal Force" node from two lessons ago.
The real Atlas UI: a starfield concept map titled "The Friction Constellation," nodes rendered as stars linked by orange lines, grouped into labelled clusters (Foundations, Force Basics, Friction Types, Applications) with a colour-coded legend, a mastery bar reading "11 / 28 mastered · 39%" top-right, zoom and reset controls in the corner. Camera pushes in as "Normal Force" lights up and a line traces forward to "Friction on an Incline"
Builds toward a small resolve

7
Formative MCQs — Human Body
A wrong answer isn't the end of the question. It's the start of the next one. A student marks the lungs as the organ that pumps blood — wrong, but not random — so the next question goes straight at that mix-up.
MCQ "which organ pumps blood?" answered "Lungs," shown wrong, then a diagnostic follow-up question appears addressing the heart-versus-lungs confusion
Quick, confident beat

8
Scaffolded hints — Light
We don't hand over the ladder. We hand over the first rung. A student can't explain why a pencil looks bent in a glass of water — the first hint doesn't mention refraction, it just asks what changes when light moves from air into water.
The real hint-card UI: the question sits in a TutorBot chat bubble, a peach-tinted card slides in underneath it labelled "HINT – 1 · GENTLE NUDGE" with an orange left border, reminding the student what they already know and ending on a question, never an answer. If the student is still stuck, "HINT – 2" appears with a sharper nudge, then "HINT – 3" with the full explanation — only as a last resort
Continues, same energy
